## Section 14 — Glossary

*This glossary covers the key terms used across Prarabdham. It is not a comprehensive dictionary of Jyotish — it is a practical reference for understanding what you encounter in your chart reading and across this site.*

**Ashtakavarga** — A system of evaluating planetary strength based on points contributed by each planet and the Lagna to every house. Used to assess the strength of transits and the overall support available in different areas of life.

**Ashtadasha Varga — Shodashavarga** — The sixteen divisional charts of Jyotish. Each chart divides the Rasi into a specific number of equal parts to examine a specific area of life in greater detail than the main chart alone can show.

**Antardasha** — The sub period within a Maha Dasha. Each Maha Dasha contains nine Antardashas ruled by each planet in sequence.

**Atmakaraka** — The planet that has traveled the highest degree in the birth chart. In Jaimini astrology it represents the soul's primary intention and deepest lesson in this life.

**Ayanamsa** — The difference in degrees between the tropical zodiac used in Western astrology and the sidereal zodiac used in Vedic astrology. Lahiri ayanamsa is the most widely used standard in Indian Jyotish.

**Bhava** — House. The twelve divisions of the birth chart each representing a specific area of life experience.

**Bhinnastakavarga** — The individual Ashtakavarga table for each planet — showing the specific point distribution of one planet across all twelve houses.

**Chara Karaka** — In Jaimini astrology, the seven or eight significators determined by planetary degrees in the birth chart.

**Combust — Asta** — When a planet comes within a specific degree range of the Sun it is considered combust. Its independent expression becomes absorbed into the Sun's energy.

**Dasha** — A planetary time period system. Vimshottari Dasha is the most widely used system in Jyotish — spanning 120 years across nine planetary periods beginning from the Moon's Nakshatra at birth.

**Debilitation — Neecha** — The sign in which a planet's energy is most challenged — where its natural expression meets the most friction. Not a curse or permanent weakness but a specific karmic condition asking for conscious development.

**Dig Bala** — Directional strength. Each planet has a specific house where it gains maximum directional strength.

**Divisional Chart — Varga** — A chart derived from the birth chart by dividing each Rasi into equal parts. Each divisional chart examines a specific area of life in detail.

**Dosha** — Literally means fault or blemish. Refers to specific planetary conditions that indicate areas of friction or challenge.

**Drishti — Aspect** — The influence one planet casts on another planet or house.

**Exaltation — Uccha** — The sign in which a planet's energy expresses most powerfully and clearly. Not a guaranteed blessing but a specific strength that carries its own responsibility.

**Gochara** — Transit. The current movement of planets through the zodiac and their relationship to the natal chart positions.

**Graha** — Planet. The nine Grahas of Jyotish are Surya, Chandra, Kuja, Budha, Guru, Shukra, Shani, Rahu, and Ketu.

**Jyotish** — Vedic astrology. From the Sanskrit root Jyoti meaning light. One of the six Vedangas — limbs of the Vedas.

**Kaala Sarpa Dosha** — A chart condition where all seven classical planets fall between Rahu and Ketu. Its interpretation varies significantly across different practitioners and traditions. Often overstated in popular astrology.

**Karaka** — Significator. Each planet naturally signifies specific areas of life.

**Kendra** — The four angular houses — first, fourth, seventh, and tenth. Considered the most powerful positions in the chart.

**Kona** — The three trine houses — first, fifth, and ninth. Considered the most auspicious houses in the chart.

**Kundali** — Birth chart. The complete map of planetary positions at the moment of birth calculated for the specific place of birth.

**Lagna** — Ascendant. The Rasi rising on the eastern horizon at the exact moment and place of birth.

**Maha Dasha** — The main planetary period in the Vimshottari Dasha system.

**Mangal Dosha — Chevvai Dosham** — A chart condition where Mars is placed in specific houses. Its effects are widely misunderstood and overstated in popular astrology. Classical texts list numerous conditions under which it is cancelled or reduced.

**Mooltrikona** — A specific degree range within a planet's own sign where it expresses with particular strength and clarity.

**Muhurta** — Auspicious timing. The selection of specific moments for important actions based on planetary positions and other Panchanga factors.

**Nadi Dosha** — A matching consideration where both partners share the same Nadi as determined by their birth Nakshatra. Its significance varies across traditions and must be read in context of the complete chart.

**Nakshatra** — Lunar mansion. The zodiac is divided into 27 Nakshatras each spanning 13 degrees and 20 minutes.

**Navagraha** — The nine planets of Jyotish — Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn, Rahu, and Ketu.

**Navamsa — D9** — The ninth divisional chart. Shows the soul's deeper nature, the quality of dharma and marriage, and the underlying strength of planets.

**Panchanga** — The five limbs of the Vedic calendar — Tithi, Vara, Nakshatra, Yoga, and Karana.

**Parihara** — Remedy. Specific practices, worship, charity, or lifestyle adjustments suggested to work consciously with challenging planetary conditions.

**Prarabdha Karma** — The portion of accumulated karma that has ripened and is being experienced in this specific life. The birth chart is the map of this Prarabdha.

**Pratyantardasha** — The sub sub period within an Antardasha.

**Raja Yoga** — A planetary combination indicating significant capacity for achievement, recognition, and influence.

**Rasi** — Sign. The twelve equal divisions of the zodiac each spanning 30 degrees.

**Retrograde — Vakri** — When a planet appears to move backward from Earth's perspective. Retrograde planets carry a more internalised, intensified quality of their energy.

**Sarvashtakavarga** — The combined Ashtakavarga table showing the total points across all planets for each house.

**Shodashavarga** — See Ashtadasha Varga. The sixteen divisional charts.

**Tithi** — Lunar day. One of the five limbs of the Panchanga.

**Upachaya** — The houses of growth — third, sixth, tenth, and eleventh. Malefic planets placed here tend to improve over time and with effort.

**Vimshottari Dasha** — The most widely used Dasha system in Jyotish. A 120 year cycle of nine planetary periods.

**Yoga** — A planetary combination. Specific configurations of planets that produce particular results. The presence of a Yoga indicates potential — its actual manifestation depends on the overall chart strength, Dasha periods, and conscious engagement of the person.

*Note: This glossary covers the most commonly encountered terms. Jyotish is a vast system with extensive terminology. If you encounter a term in your chart reading that is not listed here, the chart reading itself will explain its meaning in context.*

## Section 15 — Parihara

*A note before reading: Parihara means remedy — a conscious response to challenging planetary conditions. This section presents what classical Jyotish texts describe as general remedial approaches. It does not prescribe specific remedies for specific individuals. Specific parihara — particularly gemstones, homam, yantra, and intensive rituals — must only be approached with guidance from a genuinely experienced and trusted Pandit who has examined your complete chart and life situation.*

### What Parihara Actually Is

Parihara is not a shortcut around difficult karma. What parihara does — when approached honestly — is support the soul's conscious engagement with its karma. It creates conditions that make the inner work more accessible. It does not replace the work.

### What Parihara Actually Does — An Honest Example

Consider the name of this site itself — Prarabdham. An released arrow.

Once an arrow leaves the bow it cannot be called back. The Prarabdha karma of this life is exactly that — already released, already in motion. No remedy, no ritual, no gemstone, no mantra stops that arrow mid flight. Anyone who tells you otherwise is not being honest with you.

What parihara genuinely does is something more subtle and more valuable than stopping the arrow. It improves the quality of consciousness with which you watch where the arrow lands.

A person without any inner preparation — when difficulty arrives, when the arrow lands — is lost in the impact. They react, they blame, they fear, they collapse, or they harden. A person who has worked with genuine parihara — who has built some inner steadiness through mantra, through basic sadhana, through honest self reflection — when the same arrow lands, has enough conscious presence to observe it clearly, to understand what it is asking, to respond rather than react.

The arrow still lands. The karma still moves through. The difference is whether you are present enough to understand what it means — or whether you are lost somewhere in the experience, unable to find your footing.

This is the honest purpose of parihara. Not escape from karma. Consciousness within it.

### Types of Parihara

**Mantra — Japa.** Mantra is the most universally accessible form of parihara. The Navagraha mantras — one for each of the nine planets — are widely available and considered safe for general practice. Consistency matters more than volume. The traditional recommendation is to begin on the day associated with the relevant planet. 108 repetitions using a mala is the most widely practiced standard. What requires guidance: specific Beeja mantras, Tantric mantra forms, and mantra initiation for more powerful practices should only be undertaken with a qualified teacher.

**Pooja and Temple Worship — Navagraha Temples.** Temple worship is one of the most natural and accessible forms of parihara in the South Indian tradition. The nine Navagraha temples of Tamil Nadu — each presided over by one of the nine planets — are specifically visited for planetary remedies: Suryanar Kovil (Sun), Thingalur (Moon), Vaitheeswaran Kovil (Mars), Thiruvenkadu (Mercury), Alangudi (Jupiter), Kanjanur (Venus), Thirunallar (Saturn), Thirukazhukundram (Rahu), Keezhperumpallam (Ketu).

**Dana — Charity.** The practice of giving specific items associated with each planet on its designated day — grains, colours, metals, materials. Dana is most effective when given with genuine intention and without expectation of return.

**Upavasa — Fasting.** Fasting on the day associated with a specific planet is a traditional practice. Fasting does not necessarily mean complete abstention from food. People with health conditions should not fast without medical guidance regardless of astrological recommendations.

**Colour and Lifestyle Adjustments.** Wearing colours associated with a planet and making conscious lifestyle adjustments are among the simplest and most accessible forms of parihara, carrying minimal risk.

**Yantra.** A geometric diagram representing a specific planetary energy. Strong caution — Yantras should not be obtained, installed, or used without guidance from a knowledgeable and trustworthy practitioner.

**Gemstones — Ratna.** Among the most widely recommended and most commercially misused forms of parihara. **Prarabdham does not recommend specific gemstones for specific individuals under any circumstances.** Gemstone prescription requires a complete and careful examination of the entire birth chart by a genuinely experienced practitioner. Approach all gemstone recommendations with great caution and always seek a second opinion before wearing any planetary gemstone.

**Homam and Advanced Rituals.** Powerful practices with specific requirements for proper performance. Prarabdham does not describe these practices in detail — they cannot be safely or effectively undertaken without a qualified Pandit conducting them properly.

### Ashtanga Yoga as Universal Parihara

Beyond all the specific forms described above, there is one approach that is universally applicable — safe for everyone, requiring no external prescription, working on all levels simultaneously.

**Surya Namaskar — Asana.** Twelve postures moving in rhythm with the breath. A complete foundational practice accessible to most people. Daily practice — even a small number of rounds done with full attention — builds the physical foundation for everything else.

**Nadi Shodhana — Pranayama.** Alternate nostril breathing. The most widely recommended foundational Pranayama practice. Balances both energy channels, calms the nervous system. Safe for general practice without intensive guidance. Begin with five to ten minutes daily.

**Svadhyaya — Self Study.** Honest daily self reflection. Turning the attention honestly toward one's own patterns, reactions, and inner states. This costs nothing, requires no teacher, and is perhaps the most powerful parihara available.

**Pratyahara — Conscious Withdrawal.** Five to ten minutes daily of simply sitting, withdrawing attention from external stimulation, and turning it inward.

These four basic practices together constitute a complete foundational parihara for any chart condition. Beyond these basics — advanced Pranayama, Trataka, Kundalini practices, intensive meditation — a living teacher is genuinely necessary.

### How to Find a Genuine Practitioner

**What to look for:** Someone who examines your complete chart before making any recommendation. Someone who explains the reasoning behind their recommendation clearly. Someone who acknowledges uncertainty where it exists. Someone whose primary concern is your genuine wellbeing rather than the sale of a remedy.

**Red flags:** Anyone who creates fear around your chart and then immediately offers a paid remedy. Anyone who recommends an expensive gemstone at the first consultation. Anyone who guarantees specific results. Anyone who discourages a second opinion. Anyone who makes you feel dependent on their ongoing guidance.

*Final note on parihara: The most powerful remedy available to any person is conscious engagement with their own karma. Every other form of parihara supports this central work. None of it replaces it.*
